# The Enlightenment and the Birth of Social Science

## Summary
Chapter 1 from the Open University course, written by Peter Hamilton. Provides a comprehensive analytical introduction to the Enlightenment as the foundational intellectual movement that made sociology possible. Traces how the key ideas of the philosophes — reason, empiricism, science, progress, universalism, individualism, toleration — were transformed by Saint-Simon and Comte into the first explicit programs for a science of society. Includes readings on women in the salons and Montesquieu as "the first and greatest sociologist in the Enlightenment."

## Core ideas

### What was the Enlightenment?
- A broad intellectual movement centered in 18th-century France and Scotland, with contributions from Italy and Germany
- The metaphor of "the light of reason" shining into all dark recesses of ignorance and superstition
- Kant's definition: an age of Enlightenment marked by "sapere aude" — dare to know
- The French called their time "le siècle des lumières"; the concept had different meanings across countries

### Core principles of Enlightenment thought
- **Reason**: the most important human capacity; the means to establish an authoritative ethical, aesthetic, and knowledge system
- **Empiricism**: all thought and knowledge about the natural and social world is based on empirical facts observable by the senses
- **Science**: commitment to scientific methods of investigation as applicable to society as well as nature
- **Universalism**: scientific knowledge is universal, not limited to particular nations or classes
- **Progress**: natural and social conditions can be improved through the application of science and reason
- **Individualism**: the individual is the starting point for all knowledge and action; individual reason cannot be subjected to higher authority
- **Toleration**: all human beings are essentially the same despite religious or moral convictions; beliefs of other civilizations are not inherently inferior
- **Freedom**: opposition to feudal and traditional constraints on beliefs, trade, communication, social interaction, sexuality and property

### Social context
- The philosophes were mostly from the emerging middle classes of merchants and professionals, not the aristocracy
- The salons (led by women — the salonnières) were crucial spaces for intellectual exchange; women facilitated the Enlightenment but were simultaneously constrained by it
- France was predominantly rural; peasants made up the great mass of population. The Enlightenment was an urban, educated elite phenomenon
- Euro-centrism was inherent: Gibbon spoke of "the duty of a patriot" to "prefer and promote the exclusive interest of his native country"

### From Enlightenment to early sociology
- The French Revolution was seen partly as a product of Enlightenment ideas, which generated both enthusiasm and deep anxiety about social order
- The conservative reaction (Maistre, Bonald) blamed the Enlightenment for social dissolution — but even they contributed to sociological thinking by emphasizing tradition, community, and the sacred
- **Saint-Simon (1760-1825)**: saw himself as carrying the philosophes' ideas to a new plane; "the philosophy of the 18th century was critical and revolutionary; that of the 19th will be inventive and constructive." Coined the idea of a planned industrial society governed by scientists
- **Comte (1798-1857)**: first to use the term "sociology"; synthesized Montesquieu, Turgot, Condorcet and Saint-Simon. Distinguished between static and dynamic aspects of social organization. Believed sociology would discover laws of societal evolution
- Saint-Simon and Comte provided the conduit through which Enlightenment principles flowed into modern sociology

### Montesquieu as first sociologist
- "The first and the greatest sociologist in the Enlightenment" — his *De l'esprit des lois* treated society as a natural system governed by discoverable laws
- Insisted on empirical observation: "I have not drawn my principles from my prejudices, but from the nature of things"
- Combined scientific reformism with comparative analysis of social institutions

## Possible exam questions
- What are the core principles of the Enlightenment and how did they contribute to the emergence of sociology?
- How did Saint-Simon and Comte transform Enlightenment ideas into a program for a science of society?
- Why is Montesquieu considered "the first sociologist" of the Enlightenment?
- What role did women (salonnières) play in the Enlightenment, and what paradox does this present?
- How did the French Revolution create both the impetus and the anxiety that led to early sociology?
- Compare the Enlightenment's view of reason with the conservative reaction's emphasis on tradition and community

## Open questions
- To what extent was the Enlightenment a universal movement vs. a specifically Western European one?
- How does the tension between Enlightenment universalism and its actual Euro-centrism affect sociology's foundations?
- What would a sociology that took seriously the conservative critique of the Enlightenment look like?
